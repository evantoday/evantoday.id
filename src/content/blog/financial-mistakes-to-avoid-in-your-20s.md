Navigating your finances in your 20s can be overwhelming, especially in a rapidly changing economy like Indonesia's. I've been there, and I know how easy it is to slip into bad habits that can haunt you later on. Here’s a rundown of the most common financial mistakes I've seen my peers make and what I recommend doing instead.

## 1. Not Budgeting

You've probably heard it a thousand times, but budgeting is crucial. When I first started earning money, I didn't see the need for a budget. I thought, "I can keep track of my expenses in my head." Spoiler alert: I couldn’t. 

Creating a budget helps you understand where your money goes. Use apps like **Moka** or **KoinWorks** to track your income and expenses. Aim for the [50/30/20 budget rule](/blog/50-30-20-budget-rule-explained-for-indonesians/): 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings.

## 2. Ignoring Emergency Funds

Life is unpredictable. One minute, you're enjoying a meal at **Warung Nasi Goreng**, and the next, your bike breaks down. I learned this the hard way when I had to scramble for cash to fix my motorbike after it broke down during a trip to Bali.

Aim to save at least three to six months' worth of living expenses in a separate account. Consider opening a savings account with **Bank Mandiri** or **BNI** that offers good interest rates. This fund will be a lifesaver when unexpected expenses arise.

## 3. Accumulating Debt

With easy access to credit cards and online loans, it's tempting to overspend. I once maxed out my credit card trying to keep up with friends. The thrill was short-lived, and the debt lingered much longer.

Be cautious with credit. Only use it for emergencies and pay off your balance in full each month to avoid high interest rates. If you’re already in debt, check out my tips on a [debt-free journey](/blog/debt-free-journey-tips-for-indonesians/).

## 4. Neglecting Retirement Savings

You might think retirement is decades away, but starting early can make a massive difference. I didn’t start saving until I was 28, and I wished I had begun earlier. The earlier you invest, the more you benefit from compound interest.

Consider contributing to a retirement fund or investing in **SBN** (State Bonds) or mutual funds. Even small amounts can add up over time. Look into **Bareksa** for mutual fund investments tailored to your risk profile.

## 5. Living Beyond Your Means

It’s easy to get caught up in the lifestyle of your friends or social media influencers. I remember feeling pressure to spend more on dining out and traveling just to keep up. But those lavish expenses can drain your savings quickly.

Instead of splurging on expensive restaurants, check out local gems that serve delicious food without breaking the bank. Trust me, frugality doesn't mean sacrificing quality. You can find great [frugal living tips](/blog/frugal-living-tips-in-indonesia-enjoy-quality-without-compromise/) that help you enjoy life while saving money.

## 6. Not Investing

Many young adults think investing is only for the wealthy, but that couldn't be further from the truth. I was hesitant to start investing, thinking I needed a lot of money upfront. In reality, you can start with as little as IDR 100,000 with platforms like **Ajaib** or **Stockbit**.

Investing early can yield significant returns over time. Educate yourself on different investment types, from stocks to real estate. If you're unsure where to start, read about [how much money you need to start investing in Indonesia](/blog/how-much-money-do-you-need-to-start-investing-in-indonesia/).

## 7. Not Understanding Insurance

Insurance might seem like an unnecessary expense, but it’s fundamental for protecting your finances. I once ignored health insurance and ended up with a hefty hospital bill after a minor accident. I quickly learned my lesson.

Evaluate your insurance needs, whether it's health, life, or vehicle insurance. Policies like those from **AXA** or **Allianz** can provide peace of mind. Additionally, consider the importance of understanding [do you need life insurance in Indonesia](/blog/do-you-need-life-insurance-in-indonesia-a-comprehensive-guide/).

## 8. Failing to Set Financial Goals

Without clear goals, it’s easy to drift financially. I remember just going with the flow, but I didn’t know what I was saving for. A few years later, I realized I should’ve been saving for a down payment on a house.

Set specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals. Whether it's saving for a car or a vacation, having a plan keeps you motivated and focused.

## 9. Skipping Financial Education

Financial literacy is key to making informed decisions. When I first started working, I didn’t know the basics of investing or saving. I learned through trial and error, which can be costly.

Invest time in learning. Read books, follow financial blogs like [Evan Today](https://evantoday.com), and listen to podcasts. The more you know, the better equipped you’ll be to make smart financial decisions.

## 10. Not Seeking Professional Advice

Sometimes, we all need a little help. I was hesitant to consult a financial advisor because I thought I could handle everything myself. However, a professional can provide personalized advice tailored to your situation.

If your finances feel overwhelming, consider reaching out to a financial planner. They can help you with investment strategies, retirement planning, and budgeting. 

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best budgeting app for beginners in Indonesia?
I recommend using **Moka** or **KoinWorks**. Both apps are user-friendly and help you manage your finances effectively.

### How much should I save in my emergency fund?
Aim for three to six months' worth of living expenses. This fund can help you cover unexpected costs without going into debt.

### How can I start investing with a low budget?
You can start investing with as little as IDR 100,000 through platforms like **Ajaib** or **Stockbit**. They offer various investment options suitable for beginners.

### Do I really need insurance in my 20s?
Yes, insurance is crucial for protecting your finances from unexpected events. Consider health, life, and vehicle insurance based on your needs.

### What are some good financial literacy resources?
Books, blogs like [Evan Today](https://evantoday.com), and podcasts focused on personal finance can provide valuable knowledge and insights.

By avoiding these common financial mistakes in your 20s, you can set yourself up for a more secure and prosperous future. It's never too early to take control of your finances. Start today!
