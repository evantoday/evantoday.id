## Key Takeaways

- You can start investing with as little as $1 at most major US brokerages

## The Real Minimums by Investment Type

Let's cut through the confusion. Here's exactly how much you need to start investing in each type of asset in 2026:

### Individual Stocks

**Minimum: $1 (fractional shares)**

Thanks to fractional share investing, you can buy a piece of any stock for as little as $1. Want to own Amazon at $180/share but only have $20? You can buy 0.11 shares. You'll earn proportional dividends and price gains just like a full shareholder.

**Brokerages offering fractional shares:**
- Fidelity — $1 minimum per trade
- Charles Schwab — $5 minimum per trade (Schwab Stock Slices)
- Robinhood — $1 minimum per trade
- SoFi — $5 minimum per trade

### ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

**Minimum: $1 (fractional) or the price of 1 share**

ETFs trade like stocks, so you can buy fractional shares at brokerages that offer them. A single share of popular ETFs like VOO costs around $450-$500, but you can start with $1 through fractional investing.

## But Should You Start With $1?

Technically, yes. Practically, let's be smart about this.

While you can invest with $1, there are a few things you should do before investing any money:

### 1. Build a Small Emergency Fund First

Before investing a single dollar, save at least $1,000 in a high-yield savings account. This prevents you from having to sell investments at a loss when an unexpected expense hits.

Current high-yield savings accounts paying 4-5% APY:
- Marcus by Goldman Sachs
- Ally Bank
- Capital One 360 Performance Savings
- Discover Online Savings

### 2. Pay Off High-Interest Debt

If you're carrying credit card debt at 20-25% APR, paying that off is a guaranteed 20-25% return on your money. No investment can reliably beat that.

**Exception:** If your employer matches 401(k) contributions, contribute enough to get the full match even while paying off debt. A 100% match is a 100% return — better than any debt interest rate.

### 3. Then Start Investing

Once you have a small emergency fund and no high-interest debt, start investing. Don't wait until the emergency fund is "perfect" or until all student loans are paid off. Start small and increase over time.

## What Account Should You Open First?

This is probably the most common question I get from new investors. Here's my straightforward answer:

### If You're Under 50 and Employed

**Open a Roth IRA first.**

Here's why:

- Contributions grow completely tax-free
- Withdrawals in retirement are tax-free
- You can withdraw your contributions (not gains) anytime without penalty
- The 2026 contribution limit is $7,000/year ($8,000 if over 50)
- Income limit: $161,000 (single) or $240,000 (married filing jointly) for full contribution

**Exception:** If your employer offers a 401(k) with a match, contribute enough to get the full match first, then open a Roth IRA.

### If You're Self-Employed

**Open a Solo 401(k) or SEP IRA.** These allow much higher contribution limits than a regular IRA — up to $69,000/year for a Solo 401(k) in 2026.

### If You Just Want to Start Investing Today

**Open a taxable brokerage account.** No income limits, no contribution limits, no withdrawal restrictions. Just start buying index funds. You can always open tax-advantaged accounts later and transfer assets.

## How Much Should You Invest Each Month?

The "right" amount depends on your income, expenses, and goals. Here are some frameworks:

### The 50/30/20 Rule

- 50% of after-tax income: Needs (rent, food, insurance, minimums on debt)
- 30% of after-tax income: Wants (dining out, entertainment, shopping)
- 20% of after-tax income: Savings and investing

On a $60,000 salary (roughly $3,750/month after taxes), that's $750/month toward savings and investing.

### The "Pay Yourself First" Method

Before paying any bills or spending on anything discretionary, automatically move a set amount to your investment account. Start with 10% of your paycheck and increase by 1% every quarter.

### The Roth IRA Max Method

$7,000 per year divided by 12 = $583.33/month. If you can afford it, this is a great target because it maximizes your tax-free growth.

### The Bare Minimum

$25/month. It won't make you rich, but it builds the habit. And $25/month invested in an S&P 500 index fund for 30 years becomes approximately $56,000.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I really need an emergency fund before investing?

Ideally, yes. Having at least $1,000 in accessible savings prevents you from selling investments at a loss when unexpected expenses arise. That said, if you're contributing to a 401(k) with an employer match, don't skip the match to build an emergency fund — the match is too valuable. Build both simultaneously if needed.

### Is $100 enough to start investing?

Absolutely. $100 invested in a total market index fund gives you immediate exposure to thousands of companies. The amount matters less than the consistency. Someone who invests $100/month for 10 years will have approximately $18,400 (assuming 8% returns). Starting small and increasing over time is a proven strategy.

### Should I invest or pay off student loans first?

It depends on the interest rate. If your student loans are above 6-7%, prioritize paying them off aggressively. If they're below 5% (especially federal loans), it generally makes sense to invest while making minimum loan payments, since long-term stock market returns historically exceed 5%. Always get your full 401(k) employer match regardless of loan rates.

### What's the best app for beginner investors?

For pure simplicity, Robinhood or SoFi have the most beginner-friendly interfaces. For long-term, serious investing, Fidelity and Charles Schwab offer better tools, research, customer service, and fund options. I recommend starting with Fidelity — it combines ease of use with institutional-quality features, and their zero-fee index funds can't be beat.

### Can I lose money by investing small amounts?

Yes — all investing carries risk of loss. Your $100 investment could drop to $80 or even $60 during a market downturn. However, broadly diversified index funds have never failed to recover from downturns over longer time periods. The risk of investing small amounts is real but temporary. The risk of not investing at all — missing decades of compound growth — is permanent.
